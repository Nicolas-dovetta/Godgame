The thing itself

A new category, not a new title

LIFE isn't a role-playing game with an AI bolted on — it's an emergent, persistent narrative that the model authors turn by turn. The world generates its own trouble and wonder (a plague, a siege, a stranger on the west road); consequences are permanent; the town remembers what you did to it. The nearest comparables — AI Dungeon, Character.AI — proved the appetite is enormous. LIFE's difference is structure: gauges, a Grace budget, and year-end reckonings give the open-endedness a spine, and give us a monetization surface that the fiction itself justifies.

Why it's sticky

Emotion, not content

  • Permanence. The dead stay dead unless you pay the price to raise them — and what comes back may come back wrong.
  • Memory. Characters carry ~15 memories each; the town references its own history.
  • Moral weight. "Anything is possible, nothing is free." Every act chains into the next arc.
  • Shareability. The chronicle is a story worth screenshotting — the marketing is the gameplay.
The wedge

The mechanic is the meter

  • Grace = compute. The in-fiction budget the god spends each season maps one-to-one onto inference cost.
  • Selling "Grace" is selling turns — monetization the player already believes in.
  • Emotions are free forever, so a lapsed/free player is never locked out of the tender core.
  • Difficulty ramps every year → a reason to keep an active subscription running.

Current → Future

The architecture

The prototype's elegance is also its ceiling. It's a single git repo a technical user drives by hand — no UI, no accounts, and a chronicle that grows without bound until it strains the context window and the town starts to forget. Productizing means keeping the magic (living state, permanent consequences) while adding the plumbing (memory, multi-tenancy, cost control).

Where it breaks at scale

  • Context ceiling — a 325 KB chronicle can't all be read each turn; the town drifts.
  • No accounts, no UI, no mobile; assumes a git-literate player.
  • Cost is uncontrolled and invisible; no way to meter a heavy user.

The business case

Market & model

LIFE sits at the seam of interactive fiction, AI companions, and narrative RPGs. Character.AI reportedly draws tens of millions of monthly users at companion-app engagement levels; AI Dungeon showed a single AI-native narrative game can pull seven figures of players. The cadence is the cost governor

One turn per night is the free-tier default — the mortals live overnight, the god reads the morning's prayers and moves. That single design choice, plus running each turn on the cheapest Grok tier that fits it, is what makes the unit economics work. At ~30 turns/month a free player costs cents. Monthly sells more cadence and a better model, not a different product — and the daily ritual is a proven retention loop (the Wordle/streak pattern).

Cadence solves the margin; the mechanic still meters it

Capping the free tier at one turn/night turns the old "heavy users lose money" fear into a non-issue: a free player costs ~$0.11 a month on fast Grok. Monthly caps at 4 turns/day on the better model — a $12 subscriber still costs only ~$5 to serve. Grace packs cover the true bingers, and prompt caching (~90% off the stable ruleset) plus running all bookkeeping on Grok Fast keep every turn cheap.

Assumptions. Illustrative, not a forecast. Per-turn costs assume retrieval-bounded context (~20K tokens read, not the full chronicle) with the stable ruleset cached; Grok rates per xAI, July 2026. Moderation adds ~$0.001–0.005/turn (free/open classifiers). Hosting (DB, vector store, storage) is a rounding error next to inference — ~$0.50–$2 per active user per year. Single-provider on Grok is a deliberate simplification (see the risks); the owned-model path later is a fine-tuned open model trained on your own turns.

À la carte — the margin upside sits here

On top of either tier, one-off purchases: new words & experiences (settings, scenarios, tones — the survival-horror pack, the noir pack) and Grace packs (consumable turns for a binge). Go-to-market

The chronicle is the ad

This product markets itself if you let it. Every dramatic beat — "thirteen hundred of the dead walked back through the door," a resurrection that comes back a zombie because the god was over budget — is native, screenshottable content. The whole strategy is to make that content trivial to share and irresistible to click.

01

Ship the share loop first

One-tap chronicle cards and "name your chapter" moments, lightly watermarked. The game's own output becomes the acquisition channel — Reddit (r/AIDungeon, r/rpg), TikTok & Shorts, X threads of impossible god-choices.

02

Court the storytellers

Moral-dilemma gameplay is made for streaming. A creator program + Pro tools turns Twitch/YouTube playthroughs into a top-of-funnel that our competitors can't easily buy.

03

Launch on the thesis

"A game with no code, where the AI is the engine" is a Hacker News / Product Hunt story on its own. Lead the launch with the architecture as the hook, then let the emotion close it.

04

Referral in the currency

Invites grant Grace, not discounts — a viral loop denominated in the game's own budget. Community showcases and chapter-title leaderboards keep retention social.

Compliance & safety

Launchable at $0 — mature, not explicit

Choosing mature but non-explicit is what makes zero-capex compliance real: it removes the two expensive things — paid ID verification and high-risk adult payments — and keeps you on Stripe and the app stores. The whole safety stack is free or open-source, with you as the reviewer at launch. Grok is permissive, so your moderation layer is the brake, not the model.

01

Moderation — free

Screen every input and every output: OpenAI's free Moderation API plus an open safety model (Llama Guard / ShieldGemma). Log everything. ~$0.001–0.005/turn.

02

Age gate — free

Self-attestation + a Mature 17+ rating + geo-block the few restricted regions. Non-explicit means no paid ID verification — that's the money the fork just saved.

03

CSAM / NCMEC — free, mandatory

Enforce character-age in any romantic context, register as an ESP, wire the report path, and review it yourself at MVP scale. Free to run, legally required, live from turn one.

04

Payments & hosting — $0 capex

Stripe pay-as-you-go (fees only when you earn), PWA-first to skip store fees, free-tier backend. xAI's ~$175/mo free credits cover early Grok usage.

⚠️

The one place $0 carries real risk: legal

Free templates (Termly / iubenda) get you launched, but an AI product with a data-sharing deal and CSAM obligations wants a lawyer's eyes. You don't need a firm on day one — book a one-time ~$500–1.5k review the moment you have a paying month. Everything else above is safely free. Not legal advice; validate against your jurisdictions.

The honest read

Chance of success

A lovable prototype and a real business are different bets. LIFE's odds of the first are high; the second depends almost entirely on solving long-term memory and unit economics before novelty fades.

Becomes a beloved niche product with a passionate base~70%
Reaches a durable $10M+ ARR business in 3 years~25%
Becomes a category-defining breakout at Character.AI scale~8%

Even the downside has value: a striking showcase of the "AI-native game" thesis is a credible acqui-hire, and the tech (bounded long-term memory for an evolving world) generalizes well beyond games.

Unit economics — largely solved by design One-turn-per-night caps free-tier cost at cents/month; paid tiers sell more cadence. Grace metering, ~90% prompt-cache savings, and routing bookkeeping to Grok Fast / Haiku do the rest — the old "heavy users lose money" fear was a cadence problem, now fixed.
Memory & consistency A town that forgets breaks the whole promise. Bounded long-term memory over an ever-growing world is the core, unsolved technical bet.
Safety & moderation at scale Dark themes + companion dynamics is exactly the minefield that scarred AI Dungeon. Non-negotiable, expensive, and never "done."
Single-provider dependency (Grok) One vendor for generation — a price or policy change lands directly on you, and xAI's data-sharing terms shape the free tier. Mitigated over time by fine-tuning an open model on your own accumulated turns — cheap, on-voice, and yours. Your data → your model → your moat.
Retention past novelty The open question for every AI-narrative product. The gauge/reckoning structure and permanent stakes are LIFE's best answer — the endless, ramping game is the retention design.

Build the memory layer and the meter first. Everything else in this deck is only real if the town never forgets — and if it can afford to remember.
